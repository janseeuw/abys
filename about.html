About Me

Image credits: Schmidt Ocean Institute

I'm Ana Belén Yánez Suárez — a marine biologist, National Geographic Explorer, and PhD candidate at Memorial University of New Brunswick and a former EMBRSea scholar. I study coral reef ecosystems across depths, from shallow tropical reefs in the Pacific and Caribbean to deep-sea coral communities thriving in low-oxygen waters.

My fascination with corals deepened in the Galápagos, where I contributed to the first systematic characterization of deep-sea benthic fauna on unexplored seamounts. I later led coral restoration efforts and studied coral spawning in the Caribbean, followed by research on the combined effects of warming and eutrophication on Red Sea corals.

My Journey

My fascination with marine life began during childhood summers spent on the coast of Ecuador, where I was born and raised. The diverse ecosystems of the Galápagos Islands, just a short distance from my home, sparked my curiosity about the ocean's mysteries.

After completing my undergraduate studies in Biology at the Universidad San Francisco de Quito, I pursued graduate research at Scripps Institution of Oceanography, where I specialized in deep-sea ecology. My doctoral research on hydrothermal vent communities led to the discovery of several previously unknown species and earned recognition from the scientific community.

In 2018, I was honored to be named a National Geographic Explorer, which has allowed me to combine scientific research with storytelling to raise awareness about marine conservation issues and inspire the next generation of ocean advocates.

Today, I lead an international team of researchers focused on documenting marine biodiversity in understudied regions and developing innovative approaches to ocean conservation. Our work has been featured in leading scientific journals and media outlets worldwide.

When not in the field or laboratory, I am passionate about science communication and education. I regularly give talks at universities, conferences, and public events, sharing the wonders of the ocean and the importance of its conservation.

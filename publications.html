Journal Article

Biodiversity patterns in deep-sea ecosystems: A global analysis

Yánez-Suárez, A.B., Johnson, M.R., & Smith, P.T.

Journal of Marine Biology, 45(2), 112-128.

Deep-sea ecosystems represent one of the largest and least explored biomes on Earth. In this study, we compiled data from 25 years of deep-sea exploration to analyze global patterns of biodiversity in these environments. Our results reveal significant variations in species richness and composition across different deep-sea habitats, with hydrothermal vent communities showing the highest levels of endemism. We identified several biodiversity hotspots that warrant conservation attention, particularly in the Western Pacific and Southern Ocean. These findings provide a foundation for developing more effective conservation strategies for vulnerable deep-sea ecosystems in the face of increasing anthropogenic pressures.

DOI: 10.1038/jmb.2024.0112 PDF
Book Chapter

Marine Protected Areas: Design, Implementation, and Monitoring

Yánez-Suárez, A.B. & Garcia, L.M.

In R. Thompson & S. Wilson (Eds.), Handbook of Marine Conservation (pp. 78-95). Oxford University Press.

This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of marine protected areas (MPAs) as tools for ocean conservation. We discuss the scientific principles underlying effective MPA design, including considerations of size, connectivity, and representation of key habitats. The chapter also addresses the challenges of implementation, particularly in international waters and areas with competing resource uses. We present case studies from different regions to illustrate successful approaches to stakeholder engagement, enforcement, and adaptive management. Finally, we outline best practices for monitoring MPA effectiveness and evaluating conservation outcomes over time.

Journal Article

Conservation strategies for endangered marine species: A review and framework

Yánez-Suárez, A.B., Chen, H., & Williams, T.D.

Conservation Biology, 37(4), 823-841.

Marine species face numerous anthropogenic threats, including overfishing, habitat destruction, pollution, and climate change. This review synthesizes the current state of knowledge on conservation strategies for endangered marine species, with a focus on approaches that have demonstrated success across different taxonomic groups and geographic regions. We evaluate the effectiveness of various conservation tools, including protected areas, fishing regulations, habitat restoration, and ex-situ conservation. Based on this analysis, we propose an integrated framework for marine species conservation that emphasizes adaptive management, stakeholder engagement, and cross-sectoral collaboration. The framework provides a structured approach to developing and implementing conservation strategies that address both immediate threats and long-term challenges.

DOI: 10.1111/cobi.13982 PDF
Journal Article

Climate change impacts on coral reef ecosystems: Evidence from the Galápagos Islands

Yánez-Suárez, A.B., Morales, J.C., & Thompson, R.C.

Nature Climate Change, 13(8), 742-755.

Coral reefs are among the most vulnerable ecosystems to climate change, with rising ocean temperatures and acidification posing significant threats to their survival. This study presents a 10-year dataset on coral reef health and resilience in the Galápagos Islands, a region characterized by strong environmental gradients and periodic El Niño events. We documented significant declines in coral cover and biodiversity following thermal stress events, but also identified areas of unexpected resilience. Genetic analysis of coral populations in these resilient areas revealed specific genotypes with enhanced thermal tolerance. Our findings highlight the importance of identifying and protecting climate refugia as part of comprehensive conservation strategies for coral reef ecosystems in a warming ocean.

DOI: 10.1038/s41558-023-1675-y PDF
Technical Report

Status of Marine Biodiversity in the Eastern Tropical Pacific: Challenges and Opportunities

Yánez-Suárez, A.B., Gutiérrez, M., & Patel, S.

United Nations Environment Programme, Regional Seas Reports and Studies No. 219.

This technical report provides a comprehensive assessment of marine biodiversity in the Eastern Tropical Pacific region, encompassing the territorial waters of Mexico, Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia, and Ecuador. Based on extensive field surveys and literature review, we document the current status of key marine ecosystems and species, including coral reefs, mangroves, seagrass beds, and pelagic environments. The report identifies major threats to marine biodiversity in the region, including illegal fishing, coastal development, pollution, and climate change. We also highlight successful conservation initiatives and provide recommendations for strengthening regional cooperation to address transboundary challenges. This assessment serves as a baseline for monitoring future changes and evaluating the effectiveness of conservation measures in the Eastern Tropical Pacific.

Journal Article

Environmental DNA reveals hidden diversity in deep-sea communities

Yánez-Suárez, A.B., Levin, L.A., & Ramirez-Llodra, E.

Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 119(15), e2117436119.

Traditional methods for assessing biodiversity in deep-sea environments are limited by logistical challenges and sampling biases. In this study, we employed environmental DNA (eDNA) metabarcoding to characterize biodiversity in deep-sea sediments and water samples collected from the Mariana Trench and surrounding abyssal plains. Our results revealed a significantly higher diversity of organisms than previously documented using conventional sampling methods, including numerous taxa that had not been recorded in these environments. The eDNA approach detected cryptic species, rare taxa, and organisms from adjacent habitats, providing a more comprehensive view of deep-sea community composition. We demonstrate that eDNA metabarcoding is a powerful tool for biodiversity assessment in remote and understudied marine ecosystems, with important implications for conservation planning and environmental impact assessment of emerging deep-sea industries.

DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2117436119 PDF
